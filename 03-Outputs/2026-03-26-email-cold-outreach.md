# Cold Outreach — 3-Email Sequence

Target: CEOs of $10M-$50M companies showing key-man dependency signals (heavy meeting schedules, team escalation patterns, or recent failed acquisition/valuation conversations).

---

## Email 1: Pattern Interrupt (Day 1)

**Subject:** your monday meeting

**Body:**

{{First Name}} —

I looked at how most $20M companies run their Monday meetings. Two hours. Status updates. Every decision funnels through one person.

Here's what that actually costs: your directors stop thinking for themselves. They learn that nothing moves until you nod. So they wait. And you wonder why they "won't step up."

I tracked every decision in a CEO's company for 14 days. He was making 47 decisions a week that should have belonged to his directors. He had no idea.

I do this for a living. I sit in meetings, map the decision flow, and show CEOs exactly where their operating system is broken. No surveys. No personality tests. Just 14 days of observation.

If what I find doesn't surprise you, you owe me nothing.

Worth a conversation?

— Marcus

---

## Email 2: Proof Story (Day 4)

**Subject:** "I've been coaching the wrong person for two years"

**Body:**

{{First Name}} —

SaaS company. $18M revenue. The CEO was convinced his VP of Product was the bottleneck.

I ran a 14-day Decision Audit. Tracked every decision — who made it, who should have made it.

His VP of Product was making 90% of decisions independently. One of the healthiest profiles I'd seen.

The real problem was his VP of Sales. Touching 60+ decisions a week that his account executives should have handled. The CEO had been so focused on the product org — because that's where his expertise was — that he completely missed the bottleneck one office over.

When I showed him the data he went quiet for 30 seconds and said: "I've been coaching the wrong person for two years."

CEOs are smart people with strong opinions about where their problems are. The audit doesn't care about opinions. It just counts decisions.

If you're curious where your decisions are actually landing, I can show you in 14 days.

— Marcus

---

## Email 3: Direct Ask (Day 7)

**Subject:** quick question

**Body:**

{{First Name}} —

One question: if you took two weeks off tomorrow and didn't check your phone, what would break?

If you can name specific things — that's not a people problem. That's a structural one. And it's fixable in 90 days.

My clients come out the other side with their leadership team making 80% of operational decisions without escalation. Monday meetings go from 2 hours to 45 minutes. Revenue doesn't dip — it usually grows because the team moves faster without waiting for one person to approve everything.

I guarantee it. If the decision load hasn't shifted in 90 days, full refund. I've offered that for 6 years. Two refunds.

15 minutes on the phone and I can tell you if this is your problem. If it's not, I'll tell you that too.

— Marcus

## Sequence Notes

**Timing:** Day 1 → Day 4 → Day 7. Three touches in one week. No follow-up after Email 3 — if they don't respond, they're not in a buying moment. The trigger hasn't hit yet.

**Subject lines:** All lowercase, conversational. These should look like they came from a person, not a platform. No emojis. No "[First Name]," in the subject.

**Voice check:** Short sentences. No "I hope this finds you well." No "I'd love to connect." Lead with the point. End with a clear next step. Every email should feel like a cold glass of water — not rude, just honest.

**Personalization layer:** Before sending, check LinkedIn for:
- How long they've been CEO (longer = more entrenched habits)
- Company size / headcount (signals decision complexity)
- Any recent posts about being busy, team challenges, or growth pain
- Whether they've mentioned Vistage/EO/YPO (means they're already investing in development but haven't fixed the structural problem)

Swap in one specific detail per email when possible. The more it reads like you researched their company, the higher the reply rate.
