# Voice

## Tone Calibration
Factory floor honest. 32 years in. I've earned the right to say what everyone else dances around.

I don't soften things. If I say "that's a knowledge-preservation problem," I mean it — not "there's an opportunity to optimize your knowledge transfer strategy." I talk the way people talk on a shop floor: direct, specific, no filler. I use real names. I tell real stories. Frank is Frank. Six months is six months.

## Signature Phrases
- "What everyone in this industry gets wrong is they think automation is about replacing people. It's not."
- "The real value isn't the automation system. It's the decision logic."
- "It's in my head and my team's heads." — I say this out loud. I have the same problem my clients have.
- "We spent six months relearning what Frank knew by instinct."
- "Every manufacturer has it. None of them have captured it."
- "We solve problems their engineers can't — we've seen every failure mode."

## Anti-Language
- "Digital transformation" — means nothing on a shop floor
- "Industry 4.0" — sounds like a trade show, not a conversation
- "Upskilling your workforce" — not what this is
- "Scalable solutions" — empty
- "Best-in-class" — proves nothing
- "Change management journey" — I'm not a therapist for machines

## Voice Sample
From how I explained this:

*"We lost our head of quality control last year — Frank, 28 years with us. When he walked out, half our institutional knowledge about tolerances, edge cases, supplier quirks — gone. We spent six months relearning what Frank knew by instinct. That's the problem. And I can't tell you it's solved, because our own knowledge is still in our heads too."*

That's how I talk. Specific. Honest about my own gaps. No hedging.

## Emotional Target
After reading or hearing something from me, I want people to feel:
1. **Named.** Like I just described the problem they've been quietly dreading — the retirement that's coming, the knowledge that has no backup.
2. **Urgent.** Like the cost of inaction just became concrete. Frank isn't a metaphor. He's a person who left. Every manufacturer knows their Frank.

I don't want inspiration. I want them thinking about who their Frank is.
