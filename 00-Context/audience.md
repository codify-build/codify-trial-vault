# Audience

## Buyer Profile
Last client: David Park, CEO of a commercial HVAC company doing $22M. Built the company from nothing over 15 years. Smart guy. Works 65-hour weeks. Has a leadership team of 4 people who are loyal but won't make a move without his approval. David called me because he missed his daughter's college graduation for a "crisis" that turned out to be a pricing dispute his sales director could have handled. He didn't need a coach — he needed someone to show him that he'd accidentally built a company that couldn't function without him in the room.

## Failed Alternatives
- Read every leadership book on the shelf. "Good to Great," "Radical Candor," the whole collection. Knows the theory. Can't apply it because nobody's watching him in real-time.
- Hired a big-firm consultant ($80K engagement). Got a 60-page strategy deck with nice charts. Nothing changed.
- Tried a peer group (Vistage/EO). Useful for networking, not useful for fixing the actual operating rhythm of his company.
- His wife told him to "just delegate more." He tried. It lasted 2 weeks before he was back in every meeting.

## Voice of Customer
- "I know I'm the bottleneck but I don't know how to stop."
- "Every time I step back, things fall apart."
- "I've got good people but they won't step up."
- "I didn't build this company to be chained to it."
- "My business runs fine — as long as I'm there."

## The Path to Calling
David Park almost didn't call. He picked up the phone three times over six months. What finally pushed him was a board meeting where a potential acquirer said "we like the business but we can't value it with this much key-man risk." That's when he realized his 65-hour weeks weren't just costing him family time — they were literally discounting his company's valuation. The acquirer walked. David called the next morning.

This is the pattern: the buyer knows they have a problem for months before they act. They need a moment that makes the cost concrete and undeniable — not abstract ("I should delegate more") but specific ("my company just lost a deal because of me").

## Buying Trigger
A personal cost moment. Missed anniversary. Kid's event. Health scare. Doctor says blood pressure is too high. Or: they get an acquisition offer and realize the company is worthless without them in it — David Park's acquirer literally said "we can't value it with this much key-man risk." The trigger is almost never a business metric — it's the moment the personal cost of their operating style becomes undeniable.

## The Hidden Fear
The real fear underneath every [[voice]] of customer line isn't burnout or missing family events. It's this: what if I step back and the company runs better without me? Every CEO Marcus works with has a version of this terror. They say "things fall apart when I'm gone" but what they're really saying is "I need things to fall apart when I'm gone, because if they don't, what was all this for?" Their identity is fused with being essential. This is what makes the [[offer]] transformation so threatening before it becomes liberating — the [[soul]] Decision Audit doesn't just fix the business, it forces the CEO to confront who they are without the constant proving.

## Disqualifiers
- CEOs under $5M revenue. They can't afford me, and frankly their problem is usually sales, not leadership structure.
- Anyone who says "I just need someone to hold me accountable." That's not coaching, that's babysitting. I'm not interested.
- Founders who have already decided to sell within 6 months. I need 90 days minimum for the work to take root.
- People who want to be told they're doing a great job. I'm going to show you what's actually happening in your company. If that makes you uncomfortable, I'm not your guy.
- CEOs who say "I already know what the problem is" in the first conversation. 50% chance they're not coachable. The ones who are ready say some version of "I know something's wrong but I can't see it." That humility is the prerequisite. See [[soul]] for the pattern behind the two refunds.
- CEOs who want you to coach their team FOR them without being in the room. "Just tell my VPs what to do differently, I don't need to be in the room." That's not hiring a coach — that's hiring someone to blame. One CEO who got turned down for this was offended. Six months later his VP of Ops quit and cited "lack of leadership visibility" in her exit interview. If the CEO won't participate in the observation, the [[offer]] can't work.
