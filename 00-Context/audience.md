# Audience

## Buyer Profile
Mid-market manufacturers. $2M to $15M revenue. Owner-operators or plant managers who built their operation on the backs of a few indispensable people. They know who their Franks are. They've probably already lost one — or they're watching retirement dates creep up and quietly panicking.

They come to us because they've hit a problem their engineers can't solve. Not a technical problem. A knowledge problem. Their best people carry the rules in their heads and there's no backup.

## Trigger Event
A key person retires, leaves, or signals they're close. The operator realizes — sometimes for the first time — that the system works because of that person's judgment, not because of the SOPs. The SOPs don't capture what Frank knew. Nothing does.

Secondary trigger: they start pricing an automation project and realize they can't write the requirements, because the requirements live in someone's instincts.

## Failed Alternatives
- Hired an automation vendor who delivered the hardware but not the logic underneath it. System works — until an edge case hits and nobody knows what to do.
- Wrote everything down in SOPs. The SOPs describe the process. They don't capture the judgment calls — what to check when it looks off, which supplier batches run short, when to override the system.
- Sent the veteran employee to "knowledge transfer" sessions with a junior. The junior got a tour, not a transfer.
- Did nothing and hoped the person would stay. They didn't.

## Voice of Customer
- "We can't afford to lose another Frank."
- "Our SOPs don't capture what he actually knew."
- "We spent months relearning things he did by reflex."
- "I don't even know how to write the requirements for this automation because half of it is in someone's head."
- "If [name] left tomorrow, we'd be in serious trouble."

## Disqualifiers
- Manufacturers below $2M — typically not enough institutional complexity to justify the engagement.
- Operations where the knowledge-holder refuses to participate. The capture process only works with cooperation.
- Companies where the automation project is already scoped and locked — we need to get in before the system is designed, not after.

## Psychographic
They're not anti-technology. They're pro-continuity. They've seen automation projects fail because they treated people as obstacles, not sources. They want to do this right. They just haven't found anyone who's talking about the problem they actually have.
